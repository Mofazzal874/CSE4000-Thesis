1.1 Introduction

Single-image shadow removal aims to eliminate visual distortions caused by light occlusion,
restoring the scene's original illumination and appearance. This task is highly challenging
because shadows cause a severe loss of illumination intensity and high-frequency texture
details, creating a complex non-linear mapping scenario. Overcoming these degradations is
crucial for key downstream applications like autonomous driving, surveillance, medical
imaging, remote sensing, image editing, and preprocessing, where shadows severely hinder
object tracking, recognition, and segmentation. Consequently, deep learning approaches have
evolved rapidly. Early CNN methods, such as CANet, excelled at localized feature extraction
and multi-scale semantic fusion, but their restricted receptive fields limited global context
modeling [1]. GAN-based frameworks, exemplified by MaskShadowGAN, enforced
photorealistic domain alignment through dual constraints, yet they frequently suffered from
color artifacts and training instability [2]. To capture broader interactions, Transformer
architectures like ShadowFormer and HomoFormer introduced multi-head self-attention to
model long-range spatial dependencies, though they suffer from prohibitive quadratic
computational complexity on high-resolution images, [3], [4]. Most recently, diffusion models
have used progressive stochastic denoising to deliver unmatched reconstruction quality, but
their iterative sampling pathways impose heavy inference costs that impede real-time execution
[5], [6]. While lightweight and efficient architectures explicitly target resource-constrained
deployment by streamlining processing layers, they often trade off fine-grained reconstruction
fidelity for speed [6], [7]. Despite significant progress, existing methods still struggle to
simultaneously preserve texture details, maintain structural consistency, remove shadows
completely, and achieve computational efficiency, highlighting the critical need for improved
architectures.

1.3 Objectives

The primary research and engineering goals of this thesis focus on addressing the specific
limitations of existing shadow removal models through targeted structural interventions:

   •   To develop a unified network that models global scene context and handles local
       features simultaneously without encountering prohibitive memory requirements.

   •   To implement a baseline mapping function that restores the under-exposed components
       of a scene by calculating a residual array as formulated in Equation 1.3 instead of
       predicting absolute pixel values, establishing stable training gradients.

   •   To reconstruct the true illumination of degraded regions by implementing the inverse
       attenuation function through a neural network defined in Equation 1.2, explicitly
       separating the treatment of shadow and non-shadow regions.

   •   To enforce illumination-invariant region matching by building a routing engine based
       on the Pearson correlation coefficient, ensuring the model matches shadow regions to
       proper non-shadow context fields regardless of light intensity changes.

   •   To correct statistical differences at the deepest semantic level by integrating a bottleneck
       normalization module that updates shadow tokens using the weighted statistics of their
       non-shadow neighbors.

   •   To preserve fine structural details and texture patterns across soft transitions by
       evaluating local feature gaps and applying target boundary corrections within a
       morphologically defined penumbra band.

The operational boundaries and constraints of this research are defined as follows:

   •   Dataset Constraints: The evaluation and training pipelines are strictly confined to

       study utilizes the original ISTD dataset [8], the illumination-adjusted ISTD+ variant [9],

       processing streams or multi-view image inputs are not supported by the current
       architecture.

   •   Illumination Limits: The architecture addresses shadows cast under consistent ambient
       and direct lighting conditions where non-shadow reference patches share semantic
       properties with the shadowed surfaces. Extreme low-light scenes, total occlusions
       lacking any structural reflectance information, or colored light sources that distort
       ambient properties are excluded from this investigation.

   •   Evaluation Framework: Architectural performance is measured based on localized
       and global reconstruction fidelity across standard spatial scales using objective, pixel-
       level color and structural metrics. Subjective human perceptual evaluations or
       downstream high-level task integration, such as direct object detection testing, are
       beyond the scope of this thesis.

2.1 Introduction

Single-image shadow removal is a vital yet difficult task in low-level computer vision. Shadows
form when an opaque object blocks light from a primary source, causing localized illumination
drops, color shifts, and a severe loss of underlying texture details. Because these distortions
corrupt the visual properties of a scene, they often degrade the performance of downstream
applications like object detection, semantic segmentation, and tracking systems. Effective
shadow restoration is therefore useful for both preprocessing pipelines and consumer image
editing software.Over the past two decades, methodologies for addressing this inverse problem
have shifted significantly. Early works relied on physical models, hand-crafted chromaticity
priors, and gradient manipulation to reconstruct unshaded regions. While mathematically direct,
these methods frequently failed when applied to multi-source lighting environments or highly
textured real-world scenes. The emergence of deep learning changed this approach, moving the
domain from rigid statistical priors to flexible, data-driven deep neural networks. To position
the contributions of this thesis, this chapter reviews the historical evolution and current
advancements in shadow removal. The discussion is structured across three paradigms:
traditional physical modeling, early deep learning methods utilizing convolutional neural
networks (CNNs) and generative adversarial networks (GANs), and modern configurations
centered around U-Net backbones and vision transformers (ViTs). Finally, we highlight
persistent limitations in existing frameworks, such as edge boundary artifacts, color imbalances,
and heavy computational overhead, to establish the research gaps this thesis resolves.

2.2 Relevant Terminology

To provide a common theoretical basis, this section briefly introduces the key concepts,
architectural components, and evaluation metrics used throughout this thesis.

Shadow Removal: The low-level image restoration process of identifying shadowed regions
and correcting their pixel values to match the illumination of the unshaded background.

Pearson Correlation: A statistical metric that evaluates the linear relationship between two
variables, used in image processing to match features based on distribution similarity.

Loss Functions: Optimization objectives used in shadow removal, including Charbonnier loss,
perceptual loss, multi-scale structural similarity (MS-SSIM), and color-space penalties to
minimize pixel error and preserve realistic color balance.

U-Net: An encoder-decoder architecture that extracts multi-scale contextual features during
downsampling and progressively restores fine spatial details through skip connections.

Vision Transformer (ViT): An architecture that applies self-attention to process images as
sequences of patch tokens, capturing long-range contextual relationships.

Spatial Attention: An attention mechanism that generates spatial weight maps to emphasize
informative regions within feature maps.

Channel Attention: A mechanism that models interdependencies across feature channels,
selectively emphasizing important semantic features.

Window-Based Self-Attention: A computationally efficient self-attention mechanism that
restricts token interactions to local non-overlapping windows.

Token Shuffle and Inverse Shuffle: Operations that rearrange spatial tokens to mix contextual
information, followed by an exact reversal to restore the original layout.

Position Recovery MLP: A feed-forward layer that restores shuffled spatial tokens to their
original positions.

Residual Learning: A learning strategy that predicts the residual (difference) instead of the full
target image.

2.3 Literature Review

Over the years, researchers have developed various methods to handle image shadow removal
and restoration. Common strategies include using convolutional neural networks, generative
adversarial networks, vision transformers, and specialized lightweight architectures. Each
approach has distinct advantages and drawbacks depending on the complexity of the scene and
resource limits. Several prominent works in this domain are detailed below:

2.3.1 Classical Method Based Shadow Removal Approaches

Traditional shadow removal methodologies operate by explicitly exploiting physical lighting
properties, intrinsic material constraints, and structural geometries to isolate illumination
variations from surface reflectance. Instead of relying on learned parameters, these approaches
focus on deriving illumination-invariant representations or estimating color transformations by
directly comparing shadowed and unshadowed regions. A prominent strategy involves
transforming images into illumination-invariant spaces, such as projecting log-chromaticity
properties to generate a one-dimensional grayscale image [11]. This invariant mapping allows
models to identify shadow boundaries by comparing gradient fields, zeroing the illumination
edges, and performing surface reintegration [11]. While this approach isolates simple intensity
drops under uniform illumination, it struggles in spatially non-uniform environments where
ambient lighting varies across the scene. To handle this non-uniformity, later methods
introduced region-based and neighbor-based relighting frameworks that analyze broader spatial
structures [12], [13]. For instance, grouping identical materials via graph-cut segmentation
enables models to calculate a continuous matte layer to adjust illumination across paired regions

                                               13
[12]. Similarly, matching luminance histograms and adjusting chromaticity channels within the
CIELAB space allows for effective color restoration without strict geometric constraints [13].
However, these region-matching methods degrade significantly if a clean, unshaded instance of
the exact same material is not present nearby in the scene. To bypass the need for exact region
pairs, alternative techniques leverage intrinsic image analysis or multi-scale boundary modeling
[14], [15]. Affine shadow formation models match shadowed patches from the core umbra
outward using pyramid reconstruction structures, though they rely on manual user scribbles to
locate reference regions [14]. In contrast, information-theoretic models formulate shadow
removal as an entropy-minimization problem, operating under the principle that illumination
borders artificially inflate the local entropy of texture intensities [15]. This statistical
optimization removes shadows without manual intervention, but it frequently misidentifies
legitimate structural changes as illumination gradients when true texture borders mirror the
properties of shadow edges.

2.3.2 CNN-Based Shadow Removal Approaches

Early deep learning methods for shadow removal mainly relied on convolutional neural
networks (CNNs) to learn the mapping from shadow images to shadow-free images. ST-CGAN
[8] jointly learned shadow detection and removal using a stacked conditional GAN framework.
Mask-ShadowGAN [2] removed the requirement for paired training data through cycle-
consistent adversarial learning with shadow masks. DSC [16] introduced direction-aware
spatial context features to better model shadow boundaries and illumination changes. DC-
ShadowNet [12] improved feature learning with domain-guided supervision, allowing effective
removal of both hard and soft shadows. G2R-ShadowNet [17] generated synthetic shadow pairs
to improve restoration quality under weak supervision. BMNet [18] further enhanced
reconstruction by incorporating boundary-aware learning to preserve edge details. Although
these CNN-based methods achieved strong performance, they mainly capture local features due
to the limited receptive field of convolutions, making it difficult to model long-range
dependencies and global illumination variations.




                                              14
2.3.3 Vision Tranformers-Based Shadow Removal Approaches

Transformer-based methods have changed the shadow removal landscape by replacing local
convolutional architectures with self-attention layers capable of capturing global image
dependencies. These architectures focus on mapping structural context across long distances to
transfer clean appearance details into shaded zones. For instance, global self-attention networks
exploit multi-channel spatial attention within a Retinex-based framework to build distinct
correlations between shadowed and lit portions of an image [3]. While this approach
successfully gathers wide contextual cues, modeling long-range interactions uniformly creates
immense computational complexity on high-resolution inputs. To reduce this computational
burden, modern frameworks adopt window-based local attention strategies combined with
localized mapping steps [4], [19]. Homogenizing non-uniform shadow distributions through
random row-shuffling operators allows local transformers to process scattered degradations
with linear complexity [4]. Alternatively, regional attention mechanisms restrict spatial
interactions by enforcing adaptive matching strictly between each shadow token and its
immediate non-shadow neighborhood, striking a balance between detail recovery and speed
[19]. Other vision transformer variants incorporate multi-branch structures, domain-guided
scans, or directional scanning vectors to handle specific texture challenges [20], [21], [22].
Cross-region transformers utilize dedicated feature-matching layers to align cross-domain
representations across spatial boundaries [20]. Spatial-transformer models integrate dual
spatial-attention modules with joint Fourier transform blocks to eliminate the need for separate
shadow detection steps, facilitating one-stage restoration [21]. Furthermore, high-resolution
processing models split inputs into patch embeddings handled by unified U-shaped networks,
blending global transformers with deterministic degradation estimators to limit block artifacts
[22]. Despite these structural variations, all self-attention methods remain constrained by a
fundamental trade-off, as expanding the token receptive field to improve color matching
inevitably inflates runtime memory demands.

2.3.4 GAN-Based Shadow Removal Approaches

Generative adversarial networks (GANs) reformulated shadow removal as an image-to-image
translation task, shifting the focus from pixel-level regression toward full perceptual

                                              15
distribution alignment. Early adversarial frameworks enforced joint detection and removal
configurations, utilizing stacked conditional architectures to predict shadow masks and clean
restorations simultaneously [8]. This joint optimization enables models to generate sharper
textures and more realistic illumination transitions than simple pixel loss constraints. To
eliminate the need for costly paired training data, unsupervised methods introduced cycle-
consistent structures to learn mapping functions directly from unpaired shadow and shadow-
free images [2], [23]. Mask-guided adversarial loops and domain-classifier networks optimize
these translation paths by isolating region properties during the generation process [2], [23].
Furthermore, incorporating recurrent attention layers within adversarial systems allows
generators to refine illumination fields progressively across multiple steps [24], [25]. Despite
their capacity to synthesize visually convincing textures, GAN-based frameworks are heavily
limited by their training behavior. Because adversarial optimization lacks deterministic
constraints, these models are prone to severe color distortions, boundary artifacts, and random
visual hallucinations, especially when handling complex real-world scenes that differ from the
training distribution.

2.3.5 Identified Research Gap

A look at past research shows a constant struggle between fixing global lighting and keeping
sharp local details. Vision transformers are great at matching colors across an entire image,
which helps fix the limits of standard CNNs, but their high computational cost forces them to
use small local windows or shrink the image too much. This breaking down of the image hurts
the overall layout and leaves messy halo artifacts around shadow edges. Plus, treating every
pixel the same way often leads to weird color shifts or overly bright spots in parts of the
background that never even had shadows.

These problems happen because of big design flaws in current transformer models, especially
when looking at the gaps in HomoFormer [4]. HomoFormer [4] uses a random token-shuffling
trick to spread out and weaken the shadow distortion across the network. However, this
shuffling happens blindly. It offers no structural guarantee that a dark, shadowed patch will
actually land inside the same local window as its perfect, unshadowed matching texture.
Because the model shuffles tokens without checking if the material or surface properties
actually match, it frequently scrambles local patterns and breaks the visual flow of the image.
Other models try to fix this by using dual-attention setups that combine global color matching
with local shape rules, but this mixes up the features. The network ends up fighting itself, trying
to balance smooth, widespread lighting changes with sharp, detailed boundary lines at the same
time. Even new models that use sparse patterns to save memory just group areas by basic visual
similarity instead of looking at the actual lighting context. This leaves a clear research gap for
a fast, simple setup that can smartly route and align features directly between dark and bright
zones. We need a way to build clean edge transitions and balanced colors across the whole scene
without demanding massive computer power.

2.4 Conclusion

This chapter reviewed the technological shift in single image shadow removal from traditional
physical models to data-driven deep networks, including CNNs, GANs, and vision
transformers. While each paradigm improves low-level image restoration, existing models
remain constrained by distinct trade-offs between global color harmony, local texture


                                               19
preservation, and computational complexity. Specifically, current state-of-the-art architectures
like HomoFormer [4] rely on random token-shuffling mechanics that diffuse shadow
degradation but offer no structural guarantee that a degraded patch will align inside a processing
window with its ideal non-shadow counterpart texture. This blind allocation breaks local visual
coherence and leaves messy penumbral edge artifacts. These persistent structural limitations
highlight a clear research gap for an efficient architecture capable of dynamically routing and
aligning features across different illumination zones without demanding massive computational
power. These empirical limits directly motivate the methodology developed in this thesis.

3.1 Introduction

This chapter presents the design and architectural implementation of the proposed framework
for single-image shadow removal. The methodology models shadow removal as an
illumination-guided feature routing problem. The core architecture utilizes a Vision
Transformer [26] backbone integrated with three primary components: an illumination-aware
region partitioning block to isolate distinct degradation zones, a dynamic feature routing engine
to align degraded tokens with structurally matching unshaded references, and a hierarchical
multi-scale restorative head to reconstruct penumbra boundaries. The following sections detail
the mathematical formulations, network block interactions, and multi-scale optimization
functions that constitute this framework.

3.3 Proposed System Architecture

The complete forward pass of the proposed framework follows an end-to-end hierarchical
encoder-decoder architecture [27], [28] that maps a degraded input tensor to a restored, shadow-
free image through a series of deterministic operational stages, as illustrated in the system
schematic in Figure 3.2. Given an input degraded RGB image 𝐼 ∈ ℝ𝐻×𝑊×3 and its
corresponding binary shadow mask 𝑀 ∈ ℝ𝐻×𝑊×1 , where a value of 1 denotes shadowed pixels
and 0 denotes the unshaded background, the primary objective is to reconstruct a clean, shadow-
free image 𝐼ˆ ∈ ℝ𝐻×𝑊×3 . As shown in Figure 3.2, the network avoids uniform token processing
to prevent background color drifting; instead, 𝐼 and 𝑀 are first concatenated along the channel
dimension and passed through the Input Projection layer to yield an initial feature map. This
tensor propagates down a hierarchical U-Net style structure through an Encoder Block

                                                22
composed of four progressive resolution levels, where each level utilizes a specialized
GuidedShuffleBlock executing Guided Shuffle Attention. Within these blocks, the feature space
breaks localized processing constraints by sequentially performing Pearson-guided correlation
routing [29], spatial shuffling mechanics [4], [30], and local window self-attention [10] to
dynamically map and link scattered shadow representations based on non-shadow guidance
fields, before passing through a Channel Attention Block (CAB) [31] and a strided
downsampling operation. At the deepest semantic layer (24 × 24 × 512), the resulting low-
resolution features enter the Bottleneck zone, which houses the Non-shadow Guided Adaptive
Normalization (NGAN) module [32]; here, spatial indices derived from the original binary
mask 𝑀 guide the system to selectively update the statistics of degraded shadow tokens using
the weighted distribution properties of their clean non-shadow neighbors based on the routing
matrix map. Following this deep alignment, the feature map enters the Decoder Block to be
progressively upsampled and processed by sequential StandardBlock layers across four
resolution levels, drawing structural details from the encoder via skip connections. At the final
high-resolution decoder stage (Level 1), the features are routed into the Texture Reference
Module (TRM) [33], which directly ingests the initial image 𝐼, the mask 𝑀, and the upsampled
deep features to inject a gated, non-shadow texture residual map into the shadow pixels,
resolving fine-grained textural deficits at the highest resolution skip-connection boundary to
prevent structural over-smoothing. Finally, the texture-refined representations pass through the
Output Projection layer and a Boundary Refinement (BR) block [34] which utilizes multi-scale
dilated depthwise convolutions to smooth out transitional penumbral lighting artifacts
exclusively along the soft shadow boundary band, producing the clean shadow-free image 𝐼ˆ. To
maintain strict tracking of structural shapes throughout this entire pipeline, the precise
transformation of tensor resolutions, channel depths, flattened token counts, and layer
hyperparameters across each consecutive step is detailed in Table 3.1.

3.4 Architecture Novelties

To overcome the roadblocks found in traditional single-image shadow removal systems, this
framework completely reorganizes and fine-tunes standard architectural components into a
specialized, task-specific layout. The core design philosophy centers on division of labor:
instead of forcing a single layer to resolve multiple structural problems simultaneously, the
network separates global illumination balancing from localized texture reconstruction. Relying
on generic, uniform processing across the entire image space, the system uses a targeted layout
where individual modules handle distinct characteristics of shadow degradation. The unique
structural components and design choices engineered to achieve these objectives are detailed in
the following subsections.

3.4.1 Pearson Correlation Guided Shadow–Non-Shadow Routing

Motivation: To effectively reconstruct shadow regions, a network must locate and retrieve
clean color and texture profiles from semantically identical, unshaded zones within the scene.
Standard self-attention [35] mechanisms distribute feature gradients uniformly across all spatial
dimensions, which dilutes the vital shadow-specific cues needed for accurate restoration. The
proposed framework resolves this limitation by introducing an illumination-guided routing [36]
mechanism that establishes explicit structural connections between degraded shadow regions
and clean non-shadow (NS) reference regions.The primary challenge in matching these distinct
regions lies in selecting an appropriate similarity metric. Shadow degradation is fundamentally
characterized by a global attenuation of feature responses relative to unshaded areas. Standard
geometric metrics like the dot product and Euclidean distance fail because they are sensitive to
vector magnitude variations caused by lighting changes. Similarly, cosine similarity fails to
maintain illumination invariance because an overall brightness drop shifts the vector's mean

                                               26
relative to the origin, artificially changing the computed angle even if the underlying structural
and textural content is identical. To overcome this bottleneck, the Pearson correlation
coefficient is chosen as the core routing metric. The Pearson correlation first mean-centers each
feature vector effectively stripping away illumination biases and mean shifts and then computes
the cosine similarity in this mean-centered space. As a result, a shadowed patch and its clean
NS counterpart yield a high correlation score even when illumination differences attenuate the
feature channels by a constant factor. This mathematical guarantee is achieved with an efficient
computational complexity of O(C) per channel dimension, outperforming more complex
invariant transformations.

3.6 Conclusion

This chapter detailed the structural layout and operational pipeline of the proposed single-image
shadow removal framework. By shifting the restoration paradigm toward an illumination-
guided spatial routing problem, the network successfully separates global lighting adjustments
from fine-grained texture recovery. The core of this architecture relies on the Pearson
Correlation Guided Routing mechanism, which establishes illumination-invariant matching
fields between degraded shadow zones and clean background references.

When integrated with the parallelized processing tracks of the Guided Shuffle Blocks and the
distribution alignments of the Non-Shadow Guided Adaptive Normalization (NGAN) module,
the framework effectively corrects severe intensity deficits without incurring heavy
computational overhead. Furthermore, high-frequency boundary artifacts and penumbral
variations are explicitly resolved through the localized spatial operations of the Texture
Reference Module (TRM) working alongside the Boundary Refinement block. Backed by a
specialized multi-task loss formulation that penalizes regional color distortions and edge-
gradient misalignments, this unified framework provides a structurally sound and
computationally efficient solution for high-fidelity scene restoration.

A detailed analysis of the failure cases shows that the proposed framework has some limitations
under highly complex illumination and texture conditions. Based on the observed
reconstruction errors, these limitations can be grouped into three main cases:

   1. Multi-surface color in consistency: When a shadow covers multiple regions with
       different background colors, the model may struggle to estimate separate illumination
       corrections for each surface. This can introduce slight color bleeding or residual artifacts
       near strong geometric boundaries.

   2. Sharp shadow boundary restoration: For high-contrast shadows on saturated
       surfaces, the network may preserve minor transition lines after restoration. This occurs
       when strong illumination loss removes fine texture information required for completely
       smooth reconstruction.

   3. Cross-material color mismatch: When shadows appear across materials with different
       reflectance properties, the model can produce small color shifts or halo effects. This
       happens because estimating the original surface appearance becomes difficult when the
       underlying material information is heavily degraded.

4.10 Analysis of the Results

PSNR provided by our model are statistically meaningful or a result of random variation.

assumes that the differences between the paired samples follow a normal distribution. To
validate this assumption, we first subjected the distribution of PSNR differences to a normality
assessment.

7.1 Summary

This thesis presents a robust, principles-based engineering approach to single-image shadow
removal by leveraging our core mathematical realization that the Pearson Correlation
Coefficient remains completely invariant under linear illumination shifts. By embedding this
fundamental statistical property directly into a customized Vision Transformer architecture, we
developed a localized token routing mechanism capable of mapping structural affinities and
matching textures across severe exposure gaps without relying on complex, error-prone
parametric modeling. Evaluated on the illumination-adjusted ISTD+ [9] dataset, our multi-stage
framework successfully balances the conflicting technical requirements of regional luminance
recovery and micro-texture retention. The network utilizes Non-Shadow Guided Adaptive
Normalization (NGAN) to balance regional feature distributions and safeguard unshaded
backgrounds from color drift, while simultaneously deploying a Texture Reference Module
(TRM) and an advanced Boundary Refinemen layer governed by a specialized to eliminate halo
artifacts. Ultimately, this architecture addresses the non-linear degradation constraints of
localized shadows, achieving a highly accurate shadow-zone SSIM of 0.9921 and a stable 5-
fold cross-validation error (𝐸𝑎𝑣𝑔 ) of 4.992 while maintaining an efficient parameter footprint
optimized for deployment in real-world computer vision systems.

7.2 Limitations

Although the proposed framework achieves strong performance under standard benchmark
evaluations, several technical limitations remain when applying it to highly unconstrained real-
world scenarios. The major constraints are summarized below:

   •   Dependency on Shadow Mask Accuracy: The framework relies on the input shadow
       mask to guide routing, feature normalization, and boundary refinement operations.

                                              93
       Incorrect masks can directly affect restoration quality, where missing shadow regions
       may remain untreated and incorrectly detected areas may introduce unnecessary
       illumination changes.

   •   Routing Matching Limitations: The Pearson Correlation Guided Routing mechanism
       depends on finding reliable non-shadow reference regions for feature transfer. In cases
       involving texture less surfaces or objects that only appear inside shadow regions, finding
       suitable reference tokens becomes difficult, which may reduce color and illumination
       accuracy.

   •   Multi-Loss Optimization Complexity: The proposed objective function combines
       several loss components to balance reconstruction accuracy, color consistency, and
       boundary preservation. However, selecting proper loss weights requires careful tuning
       to avoid conflicting optimization behavior during training.

   •   Complex Illumination Conditions: Performance may decrease under highly irregular
       lighting environments, such as overlapping shadows, multiple light sources, or complex
       outdoor illumination patterns, where estimating consistent shadow correction becomes
       more challenging.

   •   Domain Generalization Limitations: The framework is mainly designed for natural
       scene shadow removal. Specialized domains such as document shadow correction or
       inner-object shadow removal may require additional adaptation due to their different
       structural and texture characteristics.

7.3 Recommendations for Future Works

Although the proposed framework demonstrates effective shadow removal performance,
several research directions can be explored to further improve its robustness, efficiency, and
real-world applicability. The following improvements are recommended for future extensions:

   •   Automatic Shadow Detection Integration: The current framework depends on externally
       provided shadow masks for guiding the restoration process. Future work can integrate


                                                 94
    a dedicated shadow detection branch to develop a fully end-to-end system capable of
    detecting and removing shadows simultaneously.

•   Improved Cross-Domain Generalization: The model can be further extended by training
    on more diverse datasets containing indoor scenes, document images, complex lighting
    conditions, and different environmental settings to improve performance beyond natural
    outdoor scenarios.

•   Enhanced Region Matching Mechanisms: Future research can explore more adaptive
    feature-matching strategies to improve reference selection in challenging cases where
    suitable non-shadow regions are unavailable or surface textures are highly uniform.

•   Lightweight Model Optimization: Although the proposed architecture maintains
    computational efficiency, additional compression techniques such as pruning,
    quantization, or knowledge distillation can be applied to improve deployment on mobile
    and edge devices.

•   Video-Based Shadow Removal Extension: Extending the framework from single-image
    restoration to video shadow removal can be investigated by incorporating temporal
    consistency mechanisms to maintain stable results across consecutive frames.

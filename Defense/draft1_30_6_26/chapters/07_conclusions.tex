\chapter{Conclusions}

\section{Summary}
This thesis studied how to detect very small human targets in aerial disaster
imagery with a model small enough to deploy. Starting from a YOLO11m baseline, an
additive ablation measured the effect of CBAM attention, a high-resolution P2
detection scale, and a state-space neck on the C2A dataset under one training and
evaluation protocol. The P2 scale was the component that mattered: it raised
very-tiny recall and $\mathrm{AP}_{50}$ at a small parameter and latency cost. CBAM
was near-free and slightly improved operational scores while reducing model size.
The state-space neck added parameters and roughly tripled latency without improving
accuracy, and was therefore excluded. The recommended CBAM~+~P2 model is
competitive with much larger published detectors on the same data at about a third
of their parameter count.

\section{Limitations}
The study has clear limits. It uses a single dataset, C2A, which is semi-synthetic,
so the absolute numbers may not transfer directly to unseen real footage. Each
configuration was trained with a single seed in this phase, so small differences
between models are within run-to-run noise, a point reinforced by the saturated
aggregate AP. The medium-target results rest on a small number of instances and are
correspondingly uncertain. Finally, the lightweight and inference-time results
reported here are for the recommended model on the same dataset and do not yet
cover on-device measurement on drone hardware.

\section{Recommendations and Future Works}
Several extensions follow from these limits. The most important is validation on
real aerial search-and-rescue imagery and, beyond that, joint training on a
combined real corpus (for example SARD~\cite{sambolek2021sard},
HERIDAL~\cite{bozic2019heridal}, VisDrone~\cite{zhu2021visdrone}, and
Okutama-Action~\cite{barekatain2017okutama}) so that the detector generalises
past the composited C2A distribution. A multi-seed study with significance testing would firm up the
component comparisons for publication. On the deployment side, the lightweight
variant should be exported and measured on embedded hardware, and higher-resolution
inputs and slicing should be evaluated together on that target. The state-space
direction is not closed by the negative result reported here: a neck that is
designed to exploit the geometry of very small targets, rather than a generic scan,
remains worth investigating.

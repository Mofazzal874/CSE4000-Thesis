\chapter{Addressing Complex Engineering Problems and Activities}

\section{Introduction}
This chapter maps the thesis onto the complex-engineering-problem and
complex-engineering-activity attributes, showing where the work required depth of
knowledge, judgement under conflicting requirements, and integration of tools and
methods.

\section{Complex Engineering Problems Associated with the Current Thesis}
The work meets several of the attributes that characterise a complex engineering
problem. It required depth of knowledge across object-detection architectures,
attention mechanisms, and state-space models, none of which yields to routine
application. It involved conflicting requirements, higher recall of very small
targets against a fixed parameter and latency budget for airborne use, that
could not be satisfied at the same time and had to be traded deliberately, as the
per-size results in Chapter~IV show. The problem had no obvious single solution:
the effect of each component was not predictable in advance and had to be
established empirically, and one plausible addition, the state-space neck, turned
out not to help. The problem is also infrequently encountered in general practice,
since very-small-human detection in disaster imagery differs substantially from
ordinary object detection.

\section{Complex Engineering Activities Associated with the Current Thesis}
The corresponding activities were likewise non-routine. The work applied and
adapted resources (pretrained detectors, attention and state-space modules, a
composited benchmark dataset, and a single-GPU training environment) and
combined them into a controlled study whose components had to interoperate under
one protocol. It required engineering judgement to isolate variables, to hold the
training configuration fixed across models, and to keep evaluation protocols fair
so that measured differences could be attributed to the architecture. It also
involved managing an experimental process with real constraints: limited GPU
memory, which forced the batch-size ladder; long training runs on a single machine;
and the need for reproducible, provenance-tracked results so that the reported
numbers could be trusted and regenerated.

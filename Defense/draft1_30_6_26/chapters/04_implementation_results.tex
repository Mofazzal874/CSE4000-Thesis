\chapter{Implementation, Results and Discussions}

\section{Introduction}
This chapter reports how the four detector configurations of Chapter~III were
trained and evaluated, and what the measurements show. It fixes the experimental
setup, explains why each evaluation metric was chosen for this task, describes
the C2A dataset, and documents the training protocol as it actually ran,
including which early-stopping criterion ended each run. The results follow in
two parts: a quantitative comparison of the additive ablation on the C2A test
set, and a qualitative analysis of where the recommended model succeeds and
fails. The chapter closes with a comparison against published results on the
same dataset, the inference-time study, a check of the stated objectives
against the sections that fulfil them, and a cost account.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
All models were trained and evaluated on a single workstation with an NVIDIA
RTX 4070 Ti SUPER (16~GB, Ada, compute capability 8.9), an Intel Core
i7-14700K, and 128~GB of RAM, running Windows~11. The software stack was
Python~3.11.9, PyTorch~2.12.0 (CUDA~12.6, cuDNN~9.10), and Ultralytics~8.4.56,
with pycocotools for COCO-style evaluation. Training used one GPU with no
distributed data parallelism. Table~\ref{tab:setup} lists the training
configuration, held identical across the four models so that any measured
difference is due to the architecture and not the optimiser.

Two settings deserve their reasoning on record. The optimiser is pinned to
AdamW at $\text{lr}_0 = 0.001$ because the framework's automatic choice
(an SGD variant at $\text{lr}_0 = 0.01$) diverged on the P2-head
architecture, with the validation classification loss growing without bound
near epoch 50; AdamW trained every configuration stably. The early-stopping
patience of 50 epochs, with a stricter $F_2$-based stop at 40, follows the
convention of published small-object work rather than the framework default:
cosine-schedule training on noisy small-target data improves late, and a short
patience cuts runs before that late gain arrives.

\section{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}
Metrics were chosen for the task, not by habit, and each answers a different
question an operator or an engineer would ask of this detector.

\subsection{Operational Scores: Why F2 Leads}
In search and rescue, a missed person may never get a second look, while a
false alarm costs an operator a few seconds. The primary operating metric is
therefore the $F_2$ score, which weights recall four times as heavily as
precision. With true positives $\mathit{TP}$, false positives $\mathit{FP}$,
and false negatives $\mathit{FN}$, where a detection counts as $\mathit{TP}$
when its intersection over union with a ground-truth box reaches $0.5$
(Equation~\ref{eq:iou}), precision $P$, recall $R$, and the $F_\beta$ family
are
\begin{align}
  P = \frac{\mathit{TP}}{\mathit{TP}+\mathit{FP}}, \qquad
  R = \frac{\mathit{TP}}{\mathit{TP}+\mathit{FN}}, \qquad
  F_\beta = (1+\beta^{2})\,\frac{P \cdot R}{\beta^{2} P + R},
\end{align}
with $\beta = 1$ for the balanced $F_1$ and $\beta = 2$ for $F_2$. Operational
scores are reported at the confidence threshold that maximises each score, and
that threshold is reported too, because a deployed system must commit to one.

\subsection{Localisation Quality: AP at the Right Threshold}
Localisation is measured by COCO average precision~\cite{lin2014coco}:
$\mathrm{AP}$ averaged
over IoU thresholds $0.5$ to $0.95$, together with $\mathrm{AP}_{50}$,
$\mathrm{AP}_{75}$, and the size-partitioned $\mathrm{AP}_S$. For targets a
few pixels wide, $\mathrm{AP}_{50}$ carries most of the signal: a one-pixel
shift on an 8-px box moves its IoU far more than the same shift on an 80-px
box, so the strict thresholds inside the averaged AP mostly measure
annotation noise at this scale. The aggregate AP is still reported for
comparability, and Section~\ref{sec:quant} shows it is nearly flat across
configurations while $\mathrm{AP}_{50}$ and recall move. Average recall at
100 detections ($\mathrm{AR}_{100}$) is included because C2A scenes carry 20
to 40 people each, so a per-image detection budget of 100 is the realistic
regime.

\subsection{Per-Size Recall: the Hypothesis Test}
Recall is reported in the size bands used throughout this work (very-tiny
$<8$~px, tiny $8$--$16$~px, small $16$--$32$~px, medium $32$--$96$~px, by
$\sqrt{\text{area}}$). This is the metric that directly tests the P2
hypothesis: if a stride-4 scale helps, the gain must appear in the very-tiny
band, and nowhere else would compensate for its absence.

\subsection{Calibration and Efficiency}
An operator acts on confidence scores, so the scores must mean something. The
expected calibration error (ECE), maximum calibration error, and Brier score
quantify how far predicted confidence sits from empirical accuracy.
Efficiency is reported as parameter count, GFLOPs, and end-to-end latency
(preprocessing, forward pass, and NMS) measured on the 4070 Ti SUPER, because
the deployment claim of this thesis is stated in those units. AP-style
metrics use $\text{conf} = 0.001$ and $\text{IoU} = 0.7$; operational scores
use the per-image protocol at $\text{IoU} = 0.5$.

\section{Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}
The models are trained and tested on C2A (Combination to Application), a
dataset for human detection in disaster scenes~\cite{nihal2024c2a}. C2A was
chosen because it is the only large-scale benchmark specific to this task:
real search-and-rescue collections exist but are small (about two thousand
frames), which is not enough to train a detector of this size from a
pretrained checkpoint without severe overfitting. C2A is semi-synthetic:
disaster backgrounds from the AIDER aerial-emergency
collection~\cite{kyrkou2020aider} are combined with human poses segmented by
a U$^2$-Net model~\cite{qin2020u2net}, then randomly scaled, placed, and
annotated with horizontal boxes. The dataset contains 10{,}215 images and
roughly 360{,}000 labelled instances across four scene types (collapsed
buildings, fire, flood, and traffic accident), with 20 to 40 people per
image. The composited origin is a stated limitation: results on C2A measure
detection of very small human shapes in disaster clutter, and transfer to
fully real imagery is future work.

The frozen split used throughout is given in Table~\ref{tab:split}; split
hashes are recorded so every model sees exactly the same images.
Representative samples covering the four scene types appear in
Figure~\ref{fig:dataset_samples}, and the measured size distribution of the
test split in Figure~\ref{fig:sizedist}: $99.6\%$ of instances are smaller
than 32~px, and more than a third are below 8~px.

\section{Experimentation}
\label{sec:protocol}
The four configurations trained sequentially on the single GPU, one run at a
time, in the ablation order of Table~\ref{tab:variants}. The protocol was
built to survive an unstable power grid and to make every number reproducible.

Before each full run, an automatic smoke test pushed about one percent of the
data through two epochs, exercising every metric writer and plot path; a full
run refused to start unless a smoke pass existed from the previous 24 hours.
Training checkpoints were copied to a safety location every epoch, and a
restarted script resumed from the last checkpoint rather than beginning
again. An out-of-memory retry ladder stepped the batch size down from 16
while gradient accumulation held the effective batch at 16, which is how the
P2-head models ran at a physical batch of 8 without changing the
optimisation. A background sampler logged GPU utilisation, VRAM, power, and
temperature at two-second intervals for the full duration of every run, and
each run wrote an environment manifest (library versions, dataset and weight
hashes, seed, and hyperparameters) so that any number in this chapter can be
traced to the run that produced it.

The four runs took 7.3, 7.0, 10.2, and 29.5 hours (baseline, CBAM, CBAM+P2,
and the state-space variant). The dual early-stopping behaved differently
across them, which is itself informative. The $F_2$ stop ended the CBAM run
after its best score at epoch 239 and the CBAM+P2 run after epoch 218. The
baseline kept improving until epoch 270 and ran out the 300-epoch cap. The
state-space run peaked earliest, at epoch 149, and the fitness patience ended
it at epoch 154; its 29.5-hour wall clock, four times the baseline's, bought
no later improvement. The ablation chain uses one seed (0) throughout, per
the thesis protocol; multi-seed significance testing is deferred to a
follow-up publication, and no claim in this chapter rests on a difference
smaller than the seed noise observed in preliminary runs.

\subsection{The Additive Ablation}
Table~\ref{tab:main} reports the four configurations on the C2A test set
under the COCO protocol, and Figure~\ref{fig:waterfall} shows the same story
as a per-component waterfall. Aggregate average precision is saturated: all
four models sit within $0.002$ AP of one another, exactly the behaviour
Section~\ref{sec:metrics} anticipated for boxes this small. The differences
appear in the metrics that matter for the task. Adding the P2 head raises
$\mathrm{AP}_{50}$ from $0.843$ to $0.853$, $\mathrm{AR}_{100}$ from $0.691$
to $0.703$, and the operational $F_2$ from $0.840$ to $0.844$, at a cost of
about $0.5$~M parameters and $1$~ms of latency. CBAM on its own is close to
free: it removes about $1$~M parameters relative to the baseline, since it
replaces the two heavier C2PSA blocks, while matching or slightly improving
$F_1$/$F_2$ and giving the lowest latency of the four. The state-space neck
is the outlier in the wrong direction: it adds $2.4$~M parameters and
roughly triples end-to-end latency ($14.6 \to 41.1$~ms) without improving
any accuracy metric. On this evidence the recommended configuration is
CBAM~+~P2, and the state-space neck is not carried forward.

\subsection{Per-Size Recall}
The per-size recall in Table~\ref{tab:persize} explains the aggregate
flatness. The P2 head raises very-tiny ($<8$~px) recall from $0.743$ to
$0.757$ ($+1.5$~points), the band that matters most for spotting distant
victims, but it slightly lowers recall in the tiny, small, and medium bands.
The medium band drops most in relative terms, but it contains only 317 of the
roughly 72{,}500 test instances, so its contribution to the aggregate is
small and its estimate is noisy. The net effect is a model that is better at
the smallest targets and roughly even elsewhere, which is the intended trade
for this application. The state-space neck tracks CBAM~+~P2 almost exactly
here, confirming it adds nothing in the size regime it was meant to help; its
scan diagnostics show the module was genuinely active (the forward and
reverse scans disagree with a mean cosine distance of $0.836$), so the null
result reflects the architecture, not a wiring fault.

\subsection{Training Dynamics}
All four configurations produced training curves of the same overall shape:
losses fall steeply for roughly forty epochs, then decline slowly under the
cosine schedule while validation mAP creeps upward until each run's stopping
point. Because the curves share one trend, only one is shown, and following
the principle of reporting the least favourable case, Figure~\ref{fig:training}
gives the state-space variant, the weakest configuration of the chain. It is
also the only run with a visible incident: at epoch 28 its validation
classification loss jumped from $0.61$ to $4.5$ and validation mAP50 collapsed
to near zero, recovering over the following fifteen epochs. None of the three
convolutional configurations showed such an event, which points at the
optimisation fragility of the scan blocks rather than at the data or the
schedule. After recovery, the variant's validation mAP50 plateaus by epoch
149 despite the run continuing to epoch 154, and no late improvement appears. The other three runs follow the same
shape with the endpoints already reported in Table~\ref{tab:main}; their
stopping epochs (270, 239, and 218) are given in Section~\ref{sec:protocol}.
The precision--recall and $F_1$-against-confidence curves of the recommended
model appear in Figure~\ref{fig:curves}, and its reliability diagram and
confusion counts in Figure~\ref{fig:calib}.

The per-size results locate where the model still fails, and inspection of
the missed detections groups the failures into three recurring types. The
first is heavy occlusion, where a person is mostly hidden by debris or water
and only a fragment stays visible. The second is the extreme-scale case,
where a target spans only a few pixels and carries too little texture to
separate from the background. The third is crowding, where several people
stand close together and the detector merges them into one box.
Figure~\ref{fig:detgrid} makes the scale case concrete on a real cluster:
the stride-8 grid assigns several neighbouring people to one cell, and the
stride-4 grid separates them, which is the spatial mechanism behind the
very-tiny-recall gain of Table~\ref{tab:persize}. The residual error
concentrates in the very-tiny band, while the medium band, with few and
larger targets, is nearly saturated. The model's internal evidence for these
detections, its attention and P2 feature response on the running example, was
shown in Figure~\ref{fig:explain}.

\section{Comparison with State-of-the-Art}

\section{Inference-Time Enhancement: SAHI and TTA}
\label{sec:sahitta}
Slicing-aided hyper inference (SAHI)~\cite{akyon2022sahi} runs the trained detector
over overlapping crops so that very small targets occupy a larger fraction of each
input tile; test-time augmentation (TTA) instead runs the detector at several
resolutions and merges the results. Both are applied to the recommended CBAM~+~P2
model as separate, clearly-labelled inference-time settings, not folded into the
primary table, because the sliced protocol uses per-image box matching for which the
standard mAP is not directly comparable. Table~\ref{tab:sahi} reports the sweep,
Figure~\ref{fig:sahibars} the resulting per-size recall, and
Figure~\ref{fig:qualgrid} showed both modes on a dense scene. Among the SAHI
settings, a $256$-px slice gives the highest very-tiny recall, $0.788$ (up from
$0.758$, $+3.0$~points), but at $162$~ms per image; larger slices run faster
($54$--$113$~ms) and recover slightly less. Test-time augmentation at $1280$~px is
the most effective single setting: it raises very-tiny recall to $0.850$
($+9.2$~points) and the operational $F_2$ to $0.854$, and lifts mAP50 from $0.868$
to $0.878$ and mAP50-95 from $0.632$ to $0.677$, all at $60$~ms, cheaper than any
SAHI configuration. Beyond $1280$~px it degrades, as a model trained at $640$~px
cannot extrapolate to several times the resolution. Both remain optional
inference-time settings that trade latency for recall on the smallest targets;
neither is part of the primary model.

\section{Objectives Achieved}
\label{sec:objectives}
The four objectives stated in Chapter~I are checked here against the sections
that fulfil them.

The first objective, a compact detector built through a single-variable
additive ablation, is met by the protocol of Section~\ref{sec:protocol} and
the results of Section~\ref{sec:quant}: Table~\ref{tab:main} attributes every
change in accuracy, parameters, and latency to one architectural step, and
Figure~\ref{fig:waterfall} presents the same attribution graphically.

The second objective, selecting the configuration that best serves
very-small-target recall within a deployable budget and showing it performs
near the published state of the art, is met jointly by
Table~\ref{tab:persize}, which shows CBAM~+~P2 holding the best very-tiny
recall of the chain, and by Section~\ref{sec:sota}, where
Table~\ref{tab:sota} places the model above every published C2A baseline
except one three times its size.

The third objective, an honest test of the state-space claim, is met in
Sections~\ref{sec:quant} and~\ref{sec:protocol}: the variant's row in
Table~\ref{tab:main} records $2.4$~M added parameters and $2.8$ times the
latency for no accuracy change, its per-size behaviour in
Table~\ref{tab:persize} tracks CBAM~+~P2, and its scan diagnostics confirm
the module was active, so the negative result stands on solid measurement.

The fourth objective, raising recall at inference time without retraining,
is met in Section~\ref{sec:sahitta}: Table~\ref{tab:sahi} shows test-time
augmentation lifting very-tiny recall from $0.758$ to $0.850$ at $60$~ms per
image, with the SAHI alternatives characterised alongside.

\section{Conclusion}
The additive ablation shows that the gain on C2A comes almost entirely from
the P2 detection scale, which lifts very-tiny recall and $\mathrm{AP}_{50}$
at a small cost, while CBAM contributes efficiency and the state-space neck
contributes nothing at nearly triple the latency. The recommended CBAM~+~P2
detector reaches $\mathrm{AP}_{50}$ $0.853$ and $F_2$ $0.844$ at $19.6$~M
parameters and $14.6$~ms, stands second among all published detectors on this
benchmark at a third of the leader's size, and gains a further nine points of
very-tiny recall from test-time augmentation when latency permits. Every
objective set in Chapter~I is met by a specific, numbered result in this
chapter.

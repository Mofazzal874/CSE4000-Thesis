\chapter{Societal, Health, Environment, Safety, Ethical, Legal and Cultural Issues}

\section{Introduction}
A detector intended for disaster response is deployed around vulnerable people and
operated by public agencies, so its wider implications matter as much as its
accuracy. This chapter sets out the intellectual-property, ethical, safety, legal,
societal, and environmental considerations that bear on the work.

\section{Intellectual Property Considerations}
The work builds on open-source components under permissive terms: the Ultralytics
YOLO11 framework, PyTorch, and the CBAM and state-space modules from their
respective publications, each cited in this thesis. The C2A dataset is used under
the terms set by its authors~\cite{nihal2024c2a} and is not redistributed. The
contribution of this thesis, namely the ablation design, the trained weights, and
the reported analysis, is the author's own, and any release would follow the
licences of the underlying tools.

\section{Ethical Considerations}
The system detects people, so it carries the risks common to person-detection
technology: misuse for surveillance, and harm from false negatives in a
life-critical setting. Two points shape the ethical position taken here. First, the
intended use is search and rescue, where the cost of a missed person is high; the
evaluation is therefore weighted towards recall so that the system errs towards
flagging rather than ignoring possible victims, with a human operator making the
final judgement. Second, the training data is semi-synthetic, built from composited
human poses rather than photographs of identifiable disaster victims, which avoids
using real images of people in distress during development.

\section{Safety Considerations}
The detector is an assistive tool, not an autonomous decision-maker. It is intended
to direct a human operator's attention, and its outputs should never be treated as
a definitive count of survivors. The measured failure modes, missed heavily
occluded targets and merged detections in dense clusters, are documented in
Chapter~IV so that operators understand where the system is weak. Any field use
should keep a person in the loop and should not rely on the detector as the sole
means of clearing an area.

\section{Legal Considerations}
Airborne data collection is regulated. Drone operation over populated or disaster
areas is subject to civil-aviation rules on flight permission, altitude, and
no-fly zones, and imagery of people engages data-protection expectations even in an
emergency. This thesis concerns the detection model rather than flight operations,
but any deployment would need to comply with the relevant aviation authority's
rules and with the handling requirements for imagery that contains people.

\section{Impact of the Project on Societal, Health, and Cultural Issues}
Faster location of survivors has a direct health benefit: it shortens the time to
reach injured people, which is decisive in the first hours after a disaster. The
societal value is greatest where trained search teams are scarce relative to the
affected area, which is the case in many disaster-prone regions, including
Bangladesh. Because the model is small enough to run on modest hardware, it does
not presume access to expensive infrastructure, which widens the range of agencies
that could use it.

\section{Impact of Project on the Environment and Sustainability}
The environmental cost of the work is the energy used for training. As reported in
Chapter~IV, the full set of training runs consumed on the order of $10$--$15$~kWh on
a single consumer GPU, which is modest for a deep-learning study; the deliberate
choice to reuse saved weights for evaluation rather than retrain, and to avoid a
large multi-seed sweep in this phase, kept that figure low. Favouring a smaller,
efficient model over a larger one also reduces the energy needed at inference time,
which matters for battery-powered airborne use.

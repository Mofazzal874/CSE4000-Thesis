# Humanizer — Do's and Don'ts

A concise reference for making text read as if a real person wrote it. Distilled from Wikipedia's *Signs of AI writing* (WikiProject AI Cleanup) and current editing practice. The goal is not to "beat a detector" but to write with a genuine voice. Detectors now flag text that has been run through automated humanizer tools anyway, so real editing wins.

---

## Core principle

LLMs pick the statistically most likely next word, which pulls prose toward a smooth, even, over-balanced average. Human writing is uneven: it has specifics, opinions, mixed feelings, and the odd rough edge. Restore those and the AI "voice" fades.

---

## The Don'ts (patterns to cut)

**Significance inflation.** Don't puff up importance with *stands/serves as a testament, marks a pivotal moment, reflects a broader shift, evolving landscape, indelible mark, setting the stage for.* State the plain fact instead.

**Fake depth via -ing tails.** Avoid tacking on *...highlighting its importance, ...symbolizing the community's spirit, ...ensuring success.* These add words, not meaning.

**Promotional tone.** Cut *nestled in the heart of, boasts a, vibrant, rich cultural heritage, breathtaking, must-visit, renowned, stunning* — especially for places and "heritage" topics.

**Vague attribution / weasel words.** Replace *experts argue, observers have noted, studies show, it is believed that* with a named source, date, and specific claim. Vagueness is the tell; specificity is the fix.

**Copula avoidance.** Don't dodge *is/are/has* with *serves as, boasts, features, represents.* "The gallery **is** the exhibition space," not "serves as."

**Negative parallelism.** Avoid *not only... but also, it's not just X, it's Y,* and clipped tailing negations like *"no guessing," "no wasted motion."*

**Rule of three.** Don't force ideas into tidy triplets (*innovation, inspiration, and insight*) to sound comprehensive.

**Synonym cycling (elegant variation).** Don't rotate *the protagonist / the main character / the central figure / the hero* to avoid repeating a noun. Repeat the word.

**False ranges.** Skip *from X to Y* when X and Y aren't on a real scale.

**Overused AI vocabulary.** Go easy on: *delve, crucial, pivotal, tapestry, testament, underscore, showcase, foster, leverage, robust, comprehensive, intricate, interplay, landscape (abstract), align with, enhance, vibrant, seamless.*

**Glue-word transitions.** Don't open sentence after sentence with *Additionally, Moreover, Furthermore, Consequently.* One *however* is fine; a pile-up is the tell.

**Filler and hedging.** Trim *in order to → to; due to the fact that → because; at this point in time → now; it is important to note that.* Don't stack *could potentially possibly might.*

**Signposting.** Don't announce (*let's dive in, here's what you need to know, in this section we'll explore*). Just say the thing.

**Persuasive-authority tropes.** Drop *the real question is, at its core, what really matters, fundamentally, the heart of the matter* when the next sentence is just an ordinary point.

**Aphorism formulas.** Avoid *X is the language of Y, X is not a tool but a mirror, the currency of, the architecture of.* Make the concrete claim instead.

**Manufactured drama.** Don't stack short fragments for effect (*No symmetry. No nostalgia. The rules were gone.*). One punchy line is fine; a run is engineered.

**False balance.** Don't present every topic as an even pros-and-cons debate. Take a position you can defend.

**Generic upbeat endings.** Cut *the future looks bright, exciting times lie ahead, a step in the right direction.* End on a concrete fact.

**Chatbot residue.** Remove *I hope this helps, Great question!, Certainly!, You're absolutely right, let me know if...* and cutoff disclaimers (*as of my last update, while details are scarce*).

**Speculative gap-filling.** If a fact isn't known, say so or cut it. Don't invent *"maintains a low profile," "likely grew up in a middle-class household."* Never fabricate stats, quotes, or citations.

### Formatting Don'ts
- **Em dashes / en dashes (— –).** The single most reliable tell. Replace with a period, comma, colon, or parentheses. Also catch spaced ` — ` and double hyphens ` -- `.
- **Curly quotes** (" " ' '): use straight quotes where the context is plain text.
- **Emojis** decorating headings or bullets.
- **Mechanical boldface** on scattered phrases.
- **Inline-header bullet lists** (`- **Performance:** ...`).
- **Title Case Headings** — use sentence case.
- **Uniform paragraph blocks**, each the same length with a neat bow.

## The Do's

- **Vary rhythm.** Mix short, blunt sentences with longer, winding ones. Highlight the first few words of each sentence; if two in a row start the same way, change one.
- **Be specific.** One concrete anchor per section — a name, date, number, place, or real example — beats any amount of polish. (Only use specifics that are true or already implied; don't invent.)
- **Have a point of view.** React to facts instead of neutrally reporting them, where the genre allows it.
- **Prefer plain verbs.** *Decide* over *make a decision*; *is/has* over *serves as*.
- **Allow a little mess.** A genuine aside, a self-correction, an unresolved "I'm still not sure about this" reads human.
- **Read it aloud.** If you stumble, it's still stiff. This catches more than any checklist.
- **Preserve meaning.** When editing, don't add claims, change numbers, or soften/strengthen certainty. Cover everything the original covered.
- **Match the register.** For technical, legal, or reference text, neutral and plain *is* the correct human voice — don't force in jokes, first person, or opinions there.

## Subtle-imperfection rules (use with care)

When the aim is deliberately natural, human-flawed prose:

- Introduce a very small grammatical looseness roughly **1 sentence in 10** — no more.
- Let the occasional sentence be slightly less polished or a touch awkward, but never unclear.
- **Never** misspell words, merge words, or repeat the same mistake. Imperfections must be varied and near-invisible on a casual read.
- Never trade away clarity or the correctness of technical content for the sake of an imperfection.
- The result should read like a proficient writer who now and then writes one slightly rougher sentence.

---

## Quick workflow

1. **Scan** the draft for the patterns above; look for *clusters* of tells, not isolated ones.
2. **Rewrite** into a natural draft — vary length, add a specific, cut the filler.
3. **Ask:** "What still makes this sound AI-generated?" and note remaining tells.
4. **Revise**, then **scan for `—` and `–`** — any hit means it's not done.
5. **Read aloud** as a final check.

---

## Don't over-correct (false positives)

Not every "tell" means AI. On their own, these prove nothing: perfect grammar, formal vocabulary, a single em dash, one *however*, curly quotes (most editors auto-curl), mixed casual/formal register, or an unsourced claim. Real human signals worth *keeping*: oddly specific detail, mixed and unresolved feelings, dated slang or in-jokes, genuine asides, and natural variety in sentence length. When in doubt, look for a cluster before you cut.
